<h3>Somersault</h3>
<p><em>Review by Margaret Pomeranz</em></p>
<p>When an Australian film is selected for the Cannes Film Festival you sit up and take notice. The only feature film this year to get that honour was 'Somersault', the debut feature of Cate Shortland. <br/><br/>At sixteen, Heidi – Abbie Cornish – is feeling her way in the world through brief sexual encounters. When she tries it on with her single mother's live-in boyfriend and is sprung, she flees to Jindabyne where she hopes to connect with a former one-night stand.<br/><br/>Quietly, desperately attempting any connection possible she meets Joe - Sam Worthington – the son of a local farmer who is caught up in his own world of confused sexuality and loyalty. Heidi’s taken under the wing of local motel owner – Lynette Curran - and applies for a job at the local service station, where she forms a tentative friendship with Bianca - Hollie Andrew.<br/><br/>There's no doubting Cate Shortland's talent. Her vision for this film is delicate and wrenching, tentatively optimistic. I have images from the film that haunt me still, Heidi’s hands – that ultimate connecting point of us all, her vulnerable body wrapped up against the cold in her pale blue parka, the landscape of that world at the bottom of the mountains. <br/><br/>But towering over everything is the creation of real characters inhabiting a believable world, all of them floundering. The performances are stunning, Abbie Cornish makes us feel Heidi's fragility every step of the way, Sam Worthington is absolutely revelatory as Joe. But there’s also wonderful work from Erik Thomson, Lynette Curran, Hollie Andrew and Leah Purcell. <br/><br/>'Somersault' is the first film to emerge from the Aurora Script Development project. It’s an impressive beginning.</p>
<h3>Further comments</h3>
<p>Margaret: David?<br/><br/>David: It sure is. What a beautiful film this is and what a heart-wrenching film it is, because Heidi is only 16 years old and, gosh, she's learning life the hard way. And yet her journey is so real. I mean, it is so painful to see her make one mistake after another in the relationships she forms, all against this slightly bleak background of this wintry place.<br/><br/>Margaret: Visually the film is so evocative, isn't it?<br/><br/>David: It's a wonderful use of location to sort of counterpoint the mood of the drama. I think that's been beautifully achieved. All the performances - I agree with you - are just wonderful and I think it's a really fine Australian film.<br/><br/>Margaret: Isn't that nice to be able to rave again, two weeks running, about an Australian film? 4.5 stars from me for this.<br/><br/>David: 4.5 from me too.<br/></p>
